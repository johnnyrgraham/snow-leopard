Reproduction

Snow leopards are solitary cats and only associate with mates in mating season. Due to the long time spent raising cubs females will mate every second year.

Pregnancy and giving birth

Gestation(pregnancy) will last roughly 90 – 105 days and the cubs are usually born between April and June, generally the litter is 2 – 3 cubs but can range from 1 – 5 the cubs are born in a rocky shelter where the mother makes a warm nest of fur from her underbelly.

The cycle of a cub

The cubs are born in an undeveloped state and will not open their eyes until they are a week of age and are entirely dependent on their mother for the first year. Lactation will last five months however the young can eat solid food at 2 months and will be weaned at about 5 months. At about three months of age the cubs will begin to follow the mother and learn basic survival skills like hunting. The mother is solely responsible for supplying the cubs with food protection and learning in their first year of life.

Female snow leopards will reach sexual maturity at 2-3 years of age while males can take up to four years.

Being dependant for the first year of their life, the time the mothers are raising the cubs is the only prolonged social contact that snow leopards have.

What determines larger numbers and better breeding

Reproductive success is higher in areas where females can find secluded dens, and in areas where there is a large abundance of prey nearby. It is important for the safety of their young that the dens are inaccessible so they can hide their cubs from other carnivores while the mother finds food.
